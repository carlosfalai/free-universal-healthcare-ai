## THE MISSION IN ONE LINE

InstantHPI gives anyone in the world access to structured clinical reasoning — the same OPQRST method a real doctor uses — completely free, in any language, on a phone that already has Telegram.

*Source: WHO Global Health Observatory, World Bank Health Indicators 2022–2024*

The WHO threshold for minimum adequate care is 23 doctors per 10,000 people. Sub-Saharan Africa averages 2.9. Eight African countries have fewer than 1 doctor per 10,000. Brain drain makes it worse every year — medical graduates leave for North America and Europe where salaries are 20–50x higher.

InstantHPI does not replace doctors. It does something equally critical: it helps patients understand their symptoms well enough to know when they need emergency care, communicate effectively with whatever health worker is available, and receive a structured clinical summary when a doctor eventually IS reachable.

## PART 2 — METRICS THAT MATTER TO FUNDERS

### 2.1 What to Track (Privacy-Safe, No PHI)

The key constraint: we must prove impact without collecting any personally identifiable health information. Everything tracked must be aggregate and anonymous. This is achievable and actually more credible to funders because it avoids HIPAA/GDPR concerns entirely.

**Volume Metrics (the basics — every funder asks for these)**
- Total consultations completed (globally, by month)
- Consultations by country (Telegram provides country code at registration)
- Consultations by language
- Average session completion rate (started intake vs. completed full report)
- Return users (users who complete 2+ consultations — proxy for trust)
- Peak usage times (indicator of community health worker use vs. individual use)

**Quality Metrics (what WHO and Gates Foundation want)**
- Symptom category distribution — what conditions are people presenting with? (fever, chest pain, respiratory, obstetric, etc.) — this creates epidemiological signal without PHI
- Average OPQRST completion rate — how many of the 18 questions do users answer? High completion = trust
- Language diversity index — consultations across X languages shows genuine global reach
- Report utilization rate — do users who complete intake proceed to read the full report? (proxy for value)
- Time from start to full report (response speed metric)

**Impact Proxy Metrics (what differentiates us from chatbots)**
- Urgent referral rate — percentage of consultations where AI recommended immediate emergency care
- Geographic reach within countries — are we reaching rural areas, not just capital cities?
- Community health worker adoption — are CHWs using InstantHPI as a documentation tool for their own patient visits?

**The key insight for funders:** Epidemiological signal at scale. If 100,000 users in Niger are presenting with malaria-consistent symptoms in October, that is public health intelligence. That is what the WHO, CDC, and national health ministries actually want. InstantHPI becomes a passive surveillance system.

### 2.2 The Dashboard to Build

Build a public-facing impact dashboard at instanthpi.com/impact (or data.instanthpi.com) showing:

1. **Live consultation counter** (total all-time, updating in real-time)
2. **World map** — color-coded by consultation volume per country, clickable for country stats
3. **Top 10 languages** used this month
4. **Top presenting symptom categories** (no identifying info — just "respiratory 34%, fever 28%, etc.")
5. **Urgent referral rate** ("X% of users were advised to seek emergency care immediately")
6. **Month-over-month growth chart**

This dashboard does two things: (1) builds public credibility and press coverage, (2) gives any funder or government a single URL to review impact before a meeting.

## PART 3 — WHO RECOGNITION PATHWAY

### 3.1 The Global Initiative on Digital Health (GIDH)

**This is the primary entry point.** GIDH is WHO's formal network of organizations working on national digital health transformation, launched February 2024. Membership is open to any institution. This is where InstantHPI should register first.

**How to join:**
- URL: https://www.who.int/initiatives/gidh/become-involved
- Fill out the membership application form
- Membership requires demonstrating engagement with digital health and alignment with the Global Strategy on Digital Health 2020–2027
- No revenue requirement. No clinical certification required at this stage.

**Why this matters:** GIDH membership does not mean WHO endorsement of your product. But it puts InstantHPI in the WHO's formal ecosystem, gets the project onto WHO's radar, opens doors to WHO-convened events and consultations, and is a credible claim when approaching governments ("We are a registered member of the WHO Global Initiative on Digital Health").

**Timeline:** Apply within 30 days. Membership is relatively accessible.

### 3.2 The Global Initiative on AI for Health (GI-AI4H)

**The second and more ambitious pathway.** GI-AI4H was launched in July 2023 under WHO, the International Telecommunication Union (ITU), and the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO). It specifically governs AI tools in healthcare.

GI-AI4H runs evaluation programs where AI health tools are assessed against WHO ethical and technical standards. Being evaluated (even before "passing") grants significant credibility.

**What GI-AI4H looks at:**
- Safety and efficacy evidence
- Equity (does the tool work equally across languages/demographics?)
- Ethical governance
- Privacy protection
- Transparency (is the AI's reasoning explainable?)

**Contact:** The ITU/AI for Good team coordinates applications: https://aiforgood.itu.int/about-us/un-ai-actions/who/

**Timeline:** 6–18 months for initial evaluation. This is a Year 2 goal.

### 3.3 WHO Digital Health Strategy Alignment

InstantHPI maps directly onto WHO's stated priorities for Universal Health Coverage (UHC). When approaching WHO or using WHO in messaging, reference these specific programs:

- **Global Strategy on Digital Health 2020–2027** — InstantHPI embodies Goal 3: "Promote equitable access to digital health"
- **Universal Health Coverage** — InstantHPI extends primary care access to populations with no physical access to doctors
- **Primary Health Care Initiative** — InstantHPI is a first-contact tool for health concerns
- **Health Emergency Preparedness** — epidemic/outbreak early detection through aggregate symptom data

### 3.4 The WHO Recognition Ladder

| Stage | What It Is | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| GIDH Member | In WHO's formal ecosystem | Year 1, Month 1 |
| GI-AI4H Evaluation Submitted | Formal WHO/ITU review process initiated | Year 1, Month 6 |
| GI-AI4H Evaluation Completed | Credible "WHO-evaluated" status | Year 2 |
| WHO Case Study | Featured in WHO publications/reports | Year 2–3 |
| WHO Pilot Partner | Included in official WHO country pilots | Year 3–5 |

## PART 4 — GOVERNMENT PARTNERSHIPS

### 4.1 Where to Start — Highest-Need, Most-Receptive Countries

Not all governments are equal targets. The ideal first-country partner has: (a) a severe doctor shortage, (b) high mobile phone penetration, (c) a government that is actively seeking digital health solutions, and (d) a functioning national health ministry that can sign agreements.

**Tier 1 — Immediate targets (extreme need + receptive governments):**

**Rwanda**
- Physicians: 1.3 per 10,000 (improving but still critical in rural areas)
- Why receptive: Rwanda has the most advanced national digital health program in Sub-Saharan Africa. The Ministry of Health actively partners with Babyl (2M+ consultations), Zipline (drone drug delivery), and multiple AI health tools. A proven track record of government-NGO-tech collaboration.
- Entry point: Rwanda Biomedical Centre (RBC) — the government agency that approves health technology pilots

**Ethiopia**
- Physicians: 1.1 per 10,000 (population: 130 million — enormous reach)
- Why receptive: Ethiopia's Ministry of Health launched the Digital Health Blueprint in 2023 and is actively seeking digital tools for community health workers (HEWs — Health Extension Workers). 38,000 Health Extension Workers already deployed across the country who would be the natural users of InstantHPI.
- Entry point: Ethiopian Federal Ministry of Health, eHealth directorate

**Nigeria**
- Physicians: 4.0 per 10,000 (170M+ urban-connected population but severe rural gap)
- Why receptive: Nigeria's National Health Act explicitly calls for digital health integration. Lagos and Abuja have active startup ecosystems and government digital health offices.
- Entry point: Federal Ministry of Health, Digital Health division; also Nigerian eHealth Alliance

**Bangladesh**
- Physicians: 5.3 per 10,000 (170M+ population, high Telegram adoption)
- Why receptive: DGDA (Directorate General of Drug Administration) and DGHS (Directorate General of Health Services) actively engage with digital health NGOs. BRAC (world's largest NGO) operates extensively here and is a natural partner.
- Entry point: Directorate General of Health Services (DGHS)

**Philippines**
- Physicians: 6.0 per 10,000 (110M population, Telegram is common in rural areas)
- Why receptive: PhilHealth (national health insurance) and DOH (Department of Health) have active digital health initiatives. Vast rural archipelago makes remote health tools essential.
- Entry point: Department of Health, Health Information Systems division

**Tier 2 — Medium-term targets:**
- Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar (Southeast Asia, extreme rural gaps)
- DRC, Tanzania, Uganda (Sub-Saharan Africa with growing mobile penetration)
- Bolivia, Guatemala, Haiti (Latin America with indigenous-language populations where InstantHPI's multilingual capability is a differentiator)

### 4.2 What a Government Partnership Looks Like in Practice

There is a spectrum of government engagement. Start small.

**Level 1 — Letter of Support (Month 6–12)**
A health ministry issues a letter acknowledging InstantHPI as a recognized digital health tool. No integration required. Used in grant applications. Required for most WHO and Gates Foundation funding applications.
*Ask:* One page on ministry letterhead. Requires one meeting with the right official.

**Level 2 — Community Health Worker Pilot (Year 1–2)**
The government's CHW program (HEWs in Ethiopia, Barangay Health Workers in Philippines, etc.) uses InstantHPI as a documentation and triage tool during patient visits. Government provides community access, InstantHPI provides training and a monthly data report.
*What you give:* Free access, training materials in local language, monthly anonymized aggregate report showing what conditions the CHWs are encountering in their region.
*What they give:* Reach (thousands of CHWs = millions of patient touchpoints), legitimacy, data validation.

**Level 3 — National Health System Integration (Year 3–5)**
InstantHPI is listed on the national health authority's approved digital health tools list. May be promoted through official health app stores or national health platforms. Potentially integrated into national health records systems (as a structured intake feed, not a diagnostic).

### 4.3 Comparable Tools That Got Government Adoption

These are real precedents to cite when pitching governments:

- **eSanjeevani (India):** Government-owned telemedicine platform. 299 million consultations as of October 2024. Saved $3 billion USD in out-of-pocket costs. Started as a state-level pilot in Tamil Nadu before national rollout.
- **Babyl Rwanda:** Private-sector AI triage tool. Partnered with Rwanda Ministry of Health. 2M+ consultations by 2023. 40% reduction in diagnostic turnaround time. Proof that AI triage works in resource-limited settings.
- **mTibaHealth (Kenya):** Mobile health payments and consultation platform. Partnered with Kenya Ministry of Health. Now active in 5 African countries.
- **mNutrition (multiple countries):** GSMA-backed mobile nutrition service. Adopted by governments in 9 countries including Tanzania and Mozambique. Went from NGO pilot to government integration by generating clear outcome data.

### 4.4 Regulatory Landscape for AI Health Tools

The regulatory environment for AI-based health tools varies significantly. The key distinction: InstantHPI is a **health information and education tool**, not a diagnostic device. This is a critical framing.

**What InstantHPI IS:** A structured symptom documentation tool that helps users communicate their health status more clearly.

**What InstantHPI IS NOT:** A diagnostic tool, a prescribing tool, or a replacement for clinical judgment.

This framing keeps InstantHPI out of medical device regulatory frameworks in most jurisdictions. In the US, the FDA's Digital Health Center of Excellence has guidance that places conversational health education tools in a low-risk category. Most developing-nation regulators follow similar logic.

**Key regulatory frameworks to be aware of:**
- **WHO AI Ethics Guidelines (2024):** Released January 2024 for Large Multimodal Models. InstantHPI should align its public documentation to these principles: transparency, human oversight, safety, privacy.
- **African Union Digital Transformation Strategy:** Calls for member states to adopt digital health tools; having alignment with this strategy helps with African government pitches.
- **ASEAN Digital Health Framework:** Relevant for Philippines, Cambodia, Vietnam, Indonesia pitches.

**The safe framing for all markets:** "InstantHPI is a clinical education and structured documentation tool that empowers patients and community health workers to communicate more effectively with healthcare providers."

### Year 4–5 (2029–2030) — The Irreversible Phase

**Goal:** 10,000,000+ consultations. Institutionalized into multiple national health systems.

At this scale, InstantHPI generates real epidemiological intelligence:
- Real-time outbreak detection (spike in fever + respiratory symptoms in a region = alert)
- Drug demand forecasting (high malaria presentation rates in October = anticipate artemisinin demand)
- Health worker training gaps (consistent misuse of certain questions = training program needed)

This data becomes valuable enough that national governments and organizations like WHO pay for access to aggregate (non-PHI) reports. That is the sustainable funding model: the data, not the users, pays for the service.

**The "free forever" sustainability model:**
- Phase 1 (Years 1–3): Grant-funded. This is standard. Every major global health tool starts on grants.
- Phase 2 (Years 3–5): Mixed — some grants + government "white-label" contracts (governments pay for customized versions or branded versions for their CHW programs) + institutional data subscriptions (aggregate, anonymized epidemiological data sold to WHO, NGOs, pharma for public health intelligence)
- Phase 3 (Year 5+): Operationally self-sustaining through institutional data contracts, with individual user access remaining permanently free

The precedent: India's eSanjeevani was government-funded from day one. Babyl Rwanda is NGO + government funded. The model for "free at the point of care, funded by institutions" is well-established in global health.
